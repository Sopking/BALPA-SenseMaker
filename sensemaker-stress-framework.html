1. Why SenseMaker for This Investigation

The Project Atlas gap analysis has already established that easyJet's stress management approach is fundamentally misdirected: it individualises an organisational problem, relies exclusively on lagging indicators, and has no systematic method for gathering pilot experience against the HSE Management Standards. The SenseMaker engagement fills that gap directly — providing the leading indicator, worker-led evidence base that easyJet's own approach lacks.

Complexity Rationale Workplace stress in aviation is a Complex domain phenomenon. Multiple stressors interact — roster design, management culture, role conflict, commercial pressure, fatigue — and their effects are only coherent in retrospect. easyJet's own data confirms this: 51% of mental health referrals cite a combination of stressors, not a single identifiable cause. SenseMaker, developed by Dave Snowden (also the creator of Cynefin), is specifically designed for this domain. It collects micro-narratives and then lets the narrator interpret their own story through structured signifiers. This prevents researcher bias, surfaces patterns that surveys cannot detect, and produces data in the exact format needed for the HSE Management Standards gap analysis.

Critically, this engagement does something easyJet has never done: it puts the HSE Management Standards directly in the hands of pilots, letting them tell us which standard is failing them — in their own words, with their own meaning. The signifiers then quantify that self-interpretation across the whole workforce.

3. The Narrative Prompt

The prompt must be open enough not to pre-shape the story, while grounded in a specific experience rather than an abstract opinion. It should be accessible to positive as well as negative stories — a Safety-II design choice that makes participation feel less threatening and surfaces what conditions support coping as well as what degrades it.

Primary Prompting Question — displayed to participants

"Think of a recent experience at work that affected how you felt — physically, mentally, or emotionally. It could be something that drained you, something that supported you, or something that changed how you feel about the job. In a short paragraph, tell us what happened."

Alternative / follow-up: "Tell us about a time when the way your work is organised made things harder — or easier — than they needed to be."
Why this works: It does not lead toward a particular stressor. "Physically, mentally, or emotionally" invites the full range of stress manifestations. Grounded in a specific experience, not abstract opinion. The alternative prompt targets the systemic/organisational dimension directly — useful for a second story or follow-up engagement. Together they map onto the HSE principle that stress is a management problem, not an individual resilience problem.
Context Statement — shown before the prompt BALPA is collecting short stories about recent working experiences. There are no right or wrong answers — we want to understand what everyday working life at easyJet is actually like for pilots. Your story will be anonymised. This process is completely independent of easyJet's own systems, and your responses will never be shared with easyJet in a form that could identify you. This is a BALPA safety investigation under the Safety Representatives and Safety Committees Regulations 1977.

4. The Signification Framework

Signifiers are the self-interpretation instruments that participants use to index their own story immediately after telling it. The design below has five triads, seven dyads, two canvas placements, and a multiple-choice demographic layer. Total completion time: 8–12 minutes per story. Multiple stories are encouraged.

The key design constraint: every signifier must be genuinely ambiguous. If a pole feels obviously correct to most respondents, it is leading — and leading signifiers produce noise, not signal. Participants are locating meaning, not rating satisfaction.

Dyads (7) — one per HSE standard + two aviation-specific additions

Each dyad is a slider between two poles. Participants locate where their story sits — not how good or bad things are. The seven dyads map one-to-one to the six HSE Management Standards plus a recovery/rest dimension critical to aviation.

Dyad 1 — Demands

"In this situation, the demands on me were:"

Manageable — I could copeOverwhelming — I couldn't cope
HSE benchmark: employees should indicate they can cope. Data shows 90% MH LTA increase — this dyad quantifies whether demands are exceeding capacity.
Dyad 2 — Control

"In this situation, my ability to influence what happened was:"

I had real say and could make choicesI had no say — decisions were made for me
Autonomy is already the key negative detractor in YVM. This dyad quantifies the experience narrative context has identified. Karasek demand-control model: high demands + low control = primary stress mechanism.
Dyad 3 — Support

"The support available to me in this situation was:"

Excellent — I knew where to turn and got helpNon-existent — I was on my own
easyJet claims extensive support services. This dyad tests whether pilots experience them as accessible and effective — or whether provision and receipt are disconnected.
Dyad 4 — Relationships

"The working relationships involved in this story were:"

Positive and respectfulDifficult, hostile, or dismissive
24 formal incidents are the visible tip. This dyad surfaces the submerged experience — day-to-day culture that never reaches formal reporting, including management behaviour and bullying below the formal threshold.
Dyad 5 — Role Clarity

"In this situation, what was expected of me was:"

Clear — I knew exactly what I should doUnclear or contradictory — caught between competing expectations
Captures the tension between safety judgement, commercial pressure, and management expectation — the core intersection with the de-professionalisation investigation.
Dyad 6 — Change

"How well was any change involved in this story communicated and managed?"

Well communicated — I understood why and what it meant for mePoorly managed — I was left in the dark
Only displayed if the narrator's story involves organisational change — or left optional for narrators to self-select. The 2022 roster request spike may indicate change-driven disruption that this dyad can now surface.
Dyad 7 — Aviation-Specific: Recovery

"After this experience, my ability to recover was:"

I had enough time and space to recover properlyI went straight back into it with no recovery
This is the aviation-specific addition. Not a direct Management Standard, but critical: cumulative stress and fatigue interact in ways specific to high-tempo short-haul operations. Cross-referenced with FTL/rostering data, this connects the stress investigation to the safety case.

Composite Pattern Detection

Cross-referencing signifiers reveals the systemic dynamics that individual standards cannot capture alone.

The High-Demand / Low-Control Pattern

Dyad 1 (overwhelming) + Dyad 2 (no say) + Triad 2 Corner B

The Karasek demand-control model: this is the primary occupational stress mechanism. The most directly actionable pattern under the Management Standards. easyJet's autonomy data already suggests this — the SenseMaker data will quantify it in narrative context.

The Silent Suffering Pattern

MCQ didn't report + "too tired to bother" + Triad 2 Corner C

Stress becomes self-reinforcing: too drained to engage with the systems that might help. This is the point where individual harm becomes organisational risk — pilots who can't engage with reporting systems are also unlikely to report safety concerns.

The Unsupported Pilot Pattern

Triad 3 Corner C + Dyad 3 (non-existent) + MCQ "didn't think it would change anything"

Complete support system failure. Distinguishes between pilots who rely on colleagues (compensating informally) and those who are genuinely isolated. The 51% "combination of stressors" MH data likely includes many pilots in this pattern.

The Spillover Pattern

Stone S1 (family/home) + Triad 4 Corner C + MCQ affected mental health

Stress not contained to the workplace. Important for severity assessment under HSWA and the Hatton foreseeability principles. Also relevant to the roster request spike — bespoke requests are the formal expression of spillover.

The Operational Safety Pattern

Triad 4 Corner B + Dyad 1 (overwhelming) + Dyad 7 (no recovery)

The safety-critical signal: stress degrading operational performance. Cross-referencing with de-professionalisation data connects occupational stress to the CAA safety case. This is the pattern that puts this investigation squarely within CAA as well as HSE jurisdiction.

The Systemic Failure Pattern

Triad 5 Corner C + MCQ frequency (constant) + MCQ "who should hear: HSE/CAA"

The employer has fundamentally failed to manage stress — pilots don't just want change, they believe external intervention is necessary. Narrators self-directing to HSE/CAA is the strongest single indicator for regulatory escalation.

6. Engagement Methodology

Critical Design Principle — Independence The data collection must be entirely independent of easyJet's systems. Any perception that easyJet management could access individual responses — even if untrue — will suppress participation and bias the data toward what pilots think is safe to say rather than what is true. The investigation's credibility depends on this independence being visible, not just actual. This is especially important given that the "worried about consequences" response option in the MCQ will capture exactly this fear.
1

Platform and Setup

Weeks 1–2
  • Select a platform fully independent of easyJet IT: SenseMaker app (Cognitive Edge / The Cynefin Co.), a BALPA-hosted secure web form, or a purpose-built simple web tool replicating the signifier interactions.
  • If using full SenseMaker software, confirm subscription status (shared dependency with De-Professionalisation engagement).
  • Consider engaging The Cynefin Co. to review the signification framework before launch — their input on signifier ambiguity is valuable and adds credibility to the methodology.
  • Prepare participant information sheet: independence from easyJet, anonymisation process, how data will be used, right to withdraw, BALPA data protection compliance.
2

Pilot Test

Week 3
  • Run with 8–12 pilots across 2–3 bases before full launch.
  • Test for: does the prompt generate relevant stories? Are signifiers genuinely ambiguous (if everyone picks the same pole, the signifier is leading)? Does the flow feel comfortable and safe?
  • Check timing: target 8–12 minutes per story. Adjust signifier wording based on feedback — small changes can significantly affect where stories cluster.
  • Brief Company Council reps and base reps on the methodology and how to field questions from members.
3

Launch and Collection

Weeks 4–8 (offset from De-Professionalisation engagement by 2+ weeks)
  • Promote exclusively through BALPA channels: Company Council, base reps, BALPA newsletter, direct member communications. Do not allow easyJet to promote — this would compromise independence and trigger the "worried about consequences" suppression effect.
  • Member communication framing: "This is a BALPA safety investigation to understand what is actually causing stress at work — not a consultation exercise. Your story is protected. The results will be used to challenge easyJet under the HSE Management Standards."
  • Target 150–250 stories minimum for meaningful pattern analysis. Multiple stories per pilot are encouraged — different stories capture different stressors.
  • Monitor participation by base — low response from a specific base may indicate particularly low psychological safety there, or inadequate rep promotion. Investigate both possibilities.
  • Run a mid-point reminder at week 6, targeted at under-represented groups (FOs, specific bases).
4

Analysis

Weeks 9–11
  • Generate quantitative pattern maps: triad distributions, dyad histograms, stone placements, emotion selection frequencies, cross-correlations between signifiers.
  • Identify attractor patterns — where do stories cluster? What combinations of signifier responses repeat across multiple narrators?
  • Read narrative texts thematically: repeating language, situations, or experiences that the quantitative data points toward. If 40 pilots independently use words like "invisible," "pointless," or "unsafe to say," that language is evidence.
  • Conduct base and rank comparisons: where are patterns consistent across the fleet? Where do sharp differences emerge?
  • Cross-reference with the easyJet Info Return data: does the SenseMaker data explain the 90% MH LTA increase? Does it confirm the autonomy finding? Does it link the 2022 roster spike to a specific stress mechanism?
5

Reporting and Use

Week 12 onwards
  • Produce a pattern report for the Company Council — findings mapped standard-by-standard to the HSE Management Standards, with illustrative anonymised narratives for each.
  • Produce the formal gap analysis update for representations to easyJet management under SRSC Reg 4(1)(a): easyJet's existing gap analysis (January 2026) + SenseMaker narrative evidence = comprehensive case for systemic change.
  • If easyJet's response is inadequate, package for HSE complaint: quantitative signifier patterns + anonymised illustrative narratives + gap analysis against Management Standards + documentation of BALPA attempts to resolve.
  • Share a member summary — themes and what the CC is doing with findings. This is both ethically required (narrators deserve to know their stories were heard) and strategically important (demonstrates BALPA effectiveness).
  • Establish a baseline for intervention: if easyJet implements controls, a repeat engagement cycle can measure whether the patterns have shifted, standard by standard.

7. Sequencing and Dependencies

Relationship to the De-Professionalisation Engagement These two SenseMaker engagements are complementary but distinct. The de-professionalisation engagement captures professional judgement, autonomy, and the epistemic/safety dimension. This engagement captures the broader occupational health dimension mapped to the HSE Management Standards. The intersection at the Control standard is critical: the de-professionalisation investigation argues that management is removing pilots' professional autonomy — this engagement measures exactly the same phenomenon through the HSE lens. Two independent analytical frameworks providing convergent evidence is significantly stronger than one. Run this engagement second, at least two weeks after de-professionalisation, to avoid survey fatigue and maintain analytical distinctiveness.

Dependencies Checklist

  • Confirm SenseMaker subscription status — shared dependency with de-professionalisation engagement. Contact The Cynefin Co. to confirm licensing and platform access.
  • Engage The Cynefin Co. on signification framework review — their input on genuine ambiguity in triads is valuable before launch.
  • Decide sequencing — run after de-professionalisation engagement, or concurrently with clearly distinct member communications?
  • Agree launch timing with the full Company Council — ensure base reps are briefed and committed before public promotion begins.
  • Agree data retention and anonymisation policy with BALPA — GDPR compliance for narrative data is essential before any story collection begins.
  • Draft member communication — framing, independence assurance, purpose, anonymity, what happens with the data.
  • Set up and test engagement on platform — including a pilot run with 8–12 CC members or trusted base reps.
  • Set participation targets and monitoring milestones — 50 stories by week 2, 100 by week 4, 150+ by close.
  • Plan story wall — anonymised narrative display for members, as with the de-professionalisation engagement. Normalises the conversation and breaks isolation.
  • Schedule analysis and reporting dates — Company Council presentation date to work backward from.
